### [2] [Second-harmonic stabilization of a bulk photonic resonator](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.10338)
*Lindell M. Williams,Grant M. Brodnik,Scott B. Papp*

Main category: physics.optics

TL;DR: 利用二次谐波稳定技术，通过光腔中倍频模式的相互作用实现激光频率稳定，提供紧凑型高精度频率参考


<details>
  <summary>Details</summary>
Motivation: 传统真空法布里-珀罗腔系统体积大，而紧凑型光子谐振器受环境敏感度限制。需要开发一种能提高激光频率长期稳定性的新技术。

Method: 使用两个1550 nm激光器PDH检测超高Q值光子谐振器的倍频间隔模式，其中一个激光器倍频至775 nm。通过二次谐波信号(f_SH)将1550 nm激光器的绝对频率映射到电子信号。

Result: f_SH能准确代理频率漂移，通过外环光学测量验证。评估了该技术的灵敏度和噪声基底，f_SH足够灵敏以增强激光相对于谐振器的长期频率稳定性。

Abstract: The resonant modes of optical cavities provide a powerful resource for laser-frequency stabilization, underpinning high-precision metrology and coherent signal generation. Photonic resonators in which the optical mode propagates through material offer a compact alternative to vacuum Fabry-Perot cavity systems, but their performance is limited by sensitivity of the material to the ambient environment. In this work, we explore second-harmonic (SH) stabilization, which exploits the interplay of a dispersive mode structure against the strict energy conservation of second-harmonic generation. Operationally, we use two, 1550 nm lasers to PDH-detect octave-spaced resonant modes of an ultra-high-Q photonic resonator with one laser frequency-doubled to 775 nm. Under SH stabilization, the microwave frequency offset between the 1550 nm lasers, which we refer to as the SH signal ($f_{SH}$) maps the absolute frequency of the 1550 nm laser to an electronic signal. We characterize this mapping through comparison of the absolute optical frequency inference provided by $f_{SH}$ to an out-of-loop optical measurement, and our results suggest $f_{SH}$ accurately proxies frequency drift. We evaluate the sensitivity and noise floor of this technique, considering contributions from laser locking and bulk material properties, and conclude that $f_{SH}$ is sufficiently sensitive to enhance long-term laser-frequency stability with respect to the resonator. These results demonstrate SH stabilization as a useful technique that infers absolute drift, thereby enabling the increased stability of future compact, precision frequency references.
